Left in the countryside

It was 11 p.m. December 2020 when the finger oxygen monitor on the hand of Jose Jimenez, 82, started beeping, indicating his blood oxygen levels were rapidly dropping. Jose’s daughter Imelda, knew that without a fast response, her father would die from the COVID-19 infection rampaging through his body. She, alongside her sister, frantically searched for a hospital that still had open beds, but quickly found out that nearby hospitals were overflowing with other patients fighting for their lives.

“Everyone was crying and we didn't know what to do,” said Imelda as she remembered how she frantically called hospitals across the state that would admit her father. “We put my papa into the car and prayed we were making the right choice, if not, then he would die in the car.”

By the time Jose was admitted into the local branch of the Instituto Mexicano del Seguro Social (IMSS), the publicly funded and largest healthcare provider in Mexico, he was surrounded by others in similar declining conditions. A few days later, on January 9, 2021, Jose passed away from respiratory distress after saying goodbye to his children, who had to visit him in hazmat suits.

His wife Augustina, cried behind her round metal glasses that sat firmly on her stout face. She leaned all her weight on her cane as she slowly made her way to her husband of 50-years funeral.  Guty, as she was also known as, was described by family as a short but powerful woman. Within a few days of burying Jose, she too began to feel weak. She was driven 90 minutes away to a private hospital in Tepic, the capital of the state, for treatment, but ultimately was rushed into a public hospital where she had to enter alone.

Augustina was in the hospital nine days after her partner had passed. On January 18, 2021, she too passed away from COVID-19 complications, marking another devastating blow to the Jimenez-Delgado family.

Compounding Inequalities

Where to go for help?

After the election of President Andres Manuel Lopez Obrador (AMLO) in 2019, the Mexican government announced the Fourth Reformation, a plan to change parts of the country's social safety net and its programs. The healthcare services were centralized under the federal government, in the name of weeding out corruption and enhancing efficiency.

Public programs like IMSS and IMSS-Bienestar make up the largest sources of healthcare for the 133 million people living in Mexico. Combined, both programs serve 80% of Mexicans who have coverage through a public program.

Private healthcare systems, in comparison, make up a much smaller percentage and are often used by people able to pay for their services out of pocket or through insurance programs. [and is often considered higher quality care.] (SOMETHING LIKE THIS)  

University of California, San Francisco, researcher Lucia Abascal Miguel worked on a cross-disciplinary team to evaluate the COVID-19 response in Mexico. She said the government's attempts to overhaul the public healthcare system failed to address rampant inequalities in Mexico and the transition to a new system caused more harm than good.

“The Seguro Popular was dismantled, the IMSS was changed, many rules and internal operations were changed, health funding was cut, and at that moment a virus arrived that hit a country with a virtually nonexistent healthcare system and a population with many risk factors that were known early on to increase the risk of death from COVID,  it hit with full force,” said Abascal Miguel.

She said the inequality that the research team found when analyzing people who received care at a public hospital, as compared to a private hospital, was "completely unacceptable.” Mortality rates at public hospitals reached 60% as compared to 20%, even when adjusting for outside variables.

“Generally, you would expect better outcomes at a private hospital. But IMSS is one of the largest health systems in the world and you would not expect a mortality rate of up to 60%,” said Abascal Miguel. “Maybe you'd expect a small gap, the fact that in Mexico, being able to access private healthcare gives you a mortality rate three times lower than any other health service is completely unacceptable.”

Over the course of the pandemic, better funded private hospitals were widely seen as having more  supplies on hand to help treat low oxygen levels and test for new cases.

 Imelda described her parents as hard-working people from agricultural backgrounds who had to find ways to provide for their nine children. Jose worked countless hours in the region's volcanic soil growing sugar cane to provide for his children. Meanwhile, his wife Augustina Delgado ran a small shop from their home and sold a ziploc bag of ice for 10 pesos - equivalent to less than a US dollar.

The Jimenez family relied on public services because of their low-income status. Public healthcare was their only option, but reforms started by the government ended up making the system worse for people like them.

Imelda recalled how she and her sister frantically searched for finger oxygen monitors and oxygen tanks in the hope of helping their father. Supplies were either bought up already or so expensive that they were unable to afford them.

Rural isolationism

When COVID-19 began spreading from major Mexican cities into the lush farmlands situated inside the Sierra Madre mountain range, residents of the municipalities of Ahuacatlan, Ixtlan del Rio, Jala, Amatlan de Cañas, and Santa Maria del Oro began to see the public hospitals that were already full of people too poor to receive healthcare elsewhere begin to overflow with COVID-19 patients.

Doctors employed by the government moved to larger cities to support the COVID-19 response, requiring rural residents to travel further for medical attention. The infrastructure was never built for a crisis of this scale. Before the first cases of COVID-19 were reported in Mexico, researchers found rural areas had just 1.4 hospital beds per 100,000 people, compared to 18.8 in semirural areas, 34.2 in semiurban areas, and 80.4 in urban municipalities.

For many in these communities, the pandemic arrived the way Fátima Guadalupe González Vargas, a shopkeeper and taxi driver from the nearby village of Tetitlán, described it "Like when you throw a bomb and it reaches the small places last." For months, residents watched news of the virus spreading through major cities like Guadalajara and Tepic and told themselves it would not reach them. Some refused to wear masks inside Fátima's small grocery store. Others held gatherings anyway.

The virus arrived in Valle Verde and Tetitlán the way researchers predicted it would — carried by the constant movement of people between rural communities and larger cities. Field workers commuted to farms in Jala. Factory workers traveled to assembly plants in Tepic. Teachers, utility workers, and students all moved back and forth along the same narrow roads that connected these villages to the rest of Nayarit. When they came home, they brought the virus with them.

"Our town is small," said Fátima. "You'd think — if nobody comes, we won't get sick. But I remember a party where people came from Guadalajara. Within a week or two, many people were sick."

Dr. de la Mora described watching the same pattern play out at the hospital — tracking where patients came from as a way of tracing how the virus was moving through the mountains. "We'd ask patients where they were from," he said. "And we already knew what that meant."

The geography of Valle Verde made everything harder. The town sits at the end of a single road that feeds into the only highway connecting it to the rest of Nayarit. When someone got sick enough to need a hospital, that road was the only way out — and the nearest facility admitting COVID-19 patients was a 30-minute drive away at minimum.

Those who made it to a hospital found the arrival did not guarantee care. De la Mora described watching the Ixtlan del Rio IMSS hospital fill within a single week, a pace nobody had prepared for.

"There came a moment where we'd tell patients - you need to be hospitalized, but there are no beds," he said. "There are none."

The decision was made to shift patients to home care managed over the phone, placing the burden on families with no medical training and, often, no supplies.

The search for air

As a virus that could send people into respiratory distress, oxygen was one of the most important commodities for sick patients - without it ran the risk of dying from low blood oxygen levels., In larger cities, it could be sourced from medical suppliers but that simply was not available in rural Nayarit through conventional means.

"We got them from mechanic shops, from tire shops," said de la Mora.

Fátima found her own solution through desperation. Terrified that her diabetic mother would fall ill with no oxygen available, she tracked down a single tank through a nurse who knew of someone willing to sell one — at a cost of 14,000 pesos, roughly equivalent to several weeks of wages for a field worker in the region.

"I don't know if people were stealing them from hospitals and selling them," she said. "What I knew is that one tank cost me 14,000 pesos.”

Abascal Miguel said the oxygen crisis was not unique to this region. It played out across rural Mexico as a symptom of a healthcare system that had never adequately invested in its most vulnerable communities.

"Oxygen became a huge problem in Mexico, it turned into a black market," said Abascal Miguel. "People showed up at the hospital with their own oxygen tank because there was none there."

The economic pressure compounded everything. In Tetitlán, as in Valle Verde, most families lived day to day. A COVID test cost 1,500 pesos at the height of the pandemic — roughly equivalent to a week's wages for agricultural workers in the region.

"Food or a test," said Fátima, summing up the choice her faced. "That was it."

Workers who left to find jobs in cities and sent remittances home stopped being able to do so. The fields went untended. The small stores sold less because their customers had nothing to spend. Prices rose and never came back down.

"They went up," said Fátima, "and they stayed up. They never came down."

Within weeks of the December gatherings, Fátima watched her neighborhood empty out in a different way. A neighbor died. Then another. Then a man three houses down who had resources, who could have paid for a doctor, who died anyway because the hospital turned him away.

"It didn't matter if you had money or not," she said. "The disease took everyone."

Rural communities in Mexico make up about 20 percent of the total population, but an estimated 47% of rural residents live in poverty, 30 points higher than the rate in urban areas. Across Mexico, roughly 12% of the population are adults aged 60 and older, and about half are believed to live in poverty, with the percentage rising sharply in rural communities. Jose Jimenez, at 82 and recovering from aggressive prostate cancer, sat at the intersection of nearly every risk factor researchers would later identify as predictive of the worst outcomes.

"In Mexico, the more marginalized a place was, the less access it had," said Abascal Miguel. "The more rural, the more poor — the worse off you were, to put it plainly."

Reactionary versus preventative

For Dr. Ernesto de la Mora, the chaos of those months never fully leaves him. He still works out of the same consultorio in Valle Verde, seeing the same families he has known for years, but the pandemic changed something fundamental about how he understood his role.

"What happens in an unprepared healthcare system is that responsibility gets passed to the individual," he said. "It goes from 'the hospital should have oxygen' to 'you need to bring your own oxygen.' The system's responsibility gets transferred to the patient, which is completely unjust."

Individual preparation, researchers argue, is not a substitute for systemic readiness and five years later, the systems that failed Jose and Augustina Jimenez remain largely unchanged. Abascal Miguel said Mexico entered the pandemic without a functioning emergency health response plan. As of 2026, no such plan has been established.

"Pandemics are going to keep happening," she said. "There is still no emergency health fund, no pandemic identification and response plan. There was one before it was dismantled under AMLO and never replaced."

The data Abascal Miguel's team compiled showed that the inequalities COVID exposed did not create new problems so much as illuminate old ones. Testing rates, hospital bed availability, oxygen supply, and vaccination prioritization all followed the same pattern during the pandemic. Resources flowed toward wealthy and dense areas, leaving rural communities like Valle Verde to manage on their own. That pattern has not reversed, according to their findings.

Rural municipalities still have a fraction of the hospital beds found in urban centers. Chronic diseases like diabetes, hypertension, and obesity, which greatly contributed to COVID's death toll, remain undertreated in low-income communities across Mexico. Health spending as a share of GDP has not meaningfully increased.

"In Mexico, where you are born determines your health," said Abascal Miguel. "That is truly unacceptable."

Changes for a healthier future

Fátima still has the oxygen tank. It sits in a back room of the same grocery store her parents built, the same store she kept open through the worst months of the pandemic because closing it meant her family would not eat. She has not needed it and hopes she never will, but she has not gotten rid of it either.

"You say to yourself, I hope it never happens again," she said. "But it stays with you."

The conditions that made Valle Verde so vulnerable in the winter of 2020 are not unique to rural Mexico. Across the United States, rural communities face the same compound disadvantages: fewer hospitals, longer distances to care, higher rates of chronic disease, and populations living paycheck to paycheck with no margin for a medical crisis. When those vulnerabilities stack — poverty on top of age on top of illness on top of distance — the consequences follow a predictable pattern, regardless of which side of the border they play out on.

Abascal Miguel, who has studied health systems across Latin America and the United States, said the lesson of Mexico's COVID response is not only Mexico's to learn.

"The inequalities in health follow the inequalities in everything else," she said. "Someone with economic vulnerability will have a health vulnerability — because of the type of care they can or can't access, the kind of work they do, whether they can afford to stop working when they are sick. That is not a Mexican problem. That is a human problem."

Jose and Augustina are buried in Valle Verde, having been moved from an unmarked grave. Seven children, dozens of grandchildren, and a community of neighbors who knew them their whole lives continue to reckon with what their deaths meant — and whether, when the next crisis comes, anything will be different.
